Travelling around the Netherlands by train

Greetings! Here are some tips on train travel in/to/from/through the Netherlands. See also the home page for tips on train travel in Benelux in general.

Passenger train service operators


A number of different companies run passenger train services in the Netherlands. Starting with
Dutch non-high-speed and semi-high-speed trains:

  • NS Nederlandse Spoorwegen 'Dutch Railways' (domestic website, android, iOS; international website, android, iOS). The national railway company. Runs most trains in the Netherlands.
    • Intercity direct (ICd) is a semi-high-speed service within the Netherlands. (It runs on high-speed lines, but the trains are not capable of doing 200+km/h, and it has more stops than high-speed services.) International ICd is the Amsterdam – Brussels semi-high-speed service run together with NMBS/SNCB (Belgian Rail). There is also the The Hauge–Brussels InterCity service, also run together with NMBS/SNCB. The section between Schiphol Airport and Rotterdam Centraal has a surcharge (see below). This international service is often called the 'Benelux train', althought it has never been extended beyond Brussels to Luxembourg.
    • The Amsterdam – Berlin InterCity service is run together with DB (German Rail). Domestic trips has normal NS ticketing rules.
    • NS also runs the Gouda – Alphen aan den Rijn line for R-net, a brand of the provinces of Noord-Holland, Zuid-Holland, Flevoland, and Utrecht. Normal NS ticketing rules.
  • Arriva. Subsidiary of DB. Runs many regional trains. In the northwest, Dutch Arriva trains and ticketing rules extend into Weener and Leer (Ostfriesland) in Germany. (However, the railway bridge between Weener and Leer collapsed; replacement buses will run between Weener and Leer, and between Groningen and Leer, for some years to come.)
  • Blauwnet. Brand of the province of Overijssel. Trains run by Arriva (Zwolle – Emmen, Almelo – Hardenberg), or Keolis Nederland (Zwolle – Kampen, Zwolle – Enschede, Zutphen – Oldenzaal; Keolis is majority owned by SNCF (French Rail)).
  • Breng. Primarily a bus company, runs additional train services between Arnhem Centraal and Doetinchem, in parallel to Arriva's Arnhem Centraal – Doetinchem – Winterswijk service. Has the same ticketing as Arriva rules on this line.
  • Valleilijn. The Amersfoort – Ede-Wageningen train line is run by Connexxion, the parent company of Breng, and ultimately majority owned by the French state.

German and Belgian non-high-speed trains running in the Netherlands:

  • Eurobahn/Keolis Deutschland: Hengelo – Bielefeld Hauptbahnhof, Venlo – Hamm (Westfalen). The Hengelo line has two stations in the Netherlands: Hengelo, and Oldenzaal. This domestic section in the Netherlands follows Blauwnet/Keolis ticketing rules.
  • DB Regio NRW: Enschede – Münster (Westfalen) Hauptbahnhof, Enschede – Dortmund Hauptbahnhof, Heerlen – Herzogenrath. The Enschede lines have three stations in the Netherlands: Enschede, Enschede De Eschmarke, Glanerbrug. This domestic section in the Netherlands follows Blauwnet/Keolis ticketing rules. In the south, the majority of the Heerlen – Herzogenrath line is in the Netherlands (only Herzogenrath is in Germany). The domestic section in the Netherlands follows NS ticketing rules.
  • Abellio: Arnhem Centraal – Düsseldorf Hauptbahnhof. This line has two stations in the Netherlands: Arnhem Centraal and Zevenaar. This domestic section in the Netherlands follows Arriva ticketing rules (despite Abellio being an NS subsidiary).
  • NMBS/SNCB (Belgian Rail) runs two of its own local train services into the Netherlands: Roosendaal – Antwerpen-Centraal – Puurs, Maastricht – Liège-Guillemins – Hasselt. The Maastricht line has three stations in the Netherlands: Maastricht, Maastricht Randwyck, Eijsden. The domestic section in the Netherlands follows NS ticketing rules.

High-speed trains:

  • Thalys (Amsterdam – Brussels – Lille/Paris; seasonal trains to Bourg Saint-Maurice or Marseille). It has its own ticketing.
  • Eurostar (Amsterdam – Rotterdam – Brussels – London). It has its own ticketing. The trip from London is a direct service. The Eurostar train from the Netherlands stops for over an hour in Brussels, and passengers have to go through border controls at Brussels. (Eurostar usually puts passengers from the Netherlands on a Thalys service to Brussels.) Direct service from the Netherlands starts at the end of 2019 at the earliest.
  • ICE International (Amsterdam – Düsseldorf – Cologne – Frankfurt/Basel). ICE International is a consortium between NS and DB. Travel on the ICE within the Netherlands requires just a normal domestic ticket, plus a ICE surcharge (see below).
