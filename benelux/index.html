Travelling around Benelux by train

Greetings! Here are some tips on train travel in/to/from/through Belgium, The Netherlands, and Luxembourg. Belgium laid the first railway in Continental Europe in 1835 (Netherlands laid its first in 1839, and Luxembourg in 1845), and the three countries have well developed railway networks, connecting the vast majority of towns and cities. Within Benelux, rail is the fastest – and certainly the most convenient – way to travel between most larger city centres.

The following are some notes on train travel in Benelux in general (see also the country-specific pages!):

  • Some trains have dynamic pricing, especially the high-speed trains. Basically that means that the earlier you book a ticket, the better price you will get. Tickets for TGV (France – Brussels/Luxembourg), Eurostar (England – France – Belgium – Netherlands) and Thalys (France – Belgium – Netherlands/Germany) have dynamic pricing, and seat reservation is compulsory. (Seat reservation is already included in an ordinary TGV/Eurostar/Thalys ticket.) ICE (Netherlands/Belgium – Germany) and InterCity (Amsterdam – Berlin) tickets that include sectors in Germany also have dynamic pricing, but seat reservation is optional. (Cheaper tickets for ICE/InterCity/EuroCity in Germany restrict the traveller to specific trains, while full-fare tickets give flexibility.) IZY (Brussels – Paris) and Ouigo (France domestic) are lost-cost high-speed rail services; they are basically train-versions of budget airlines.
  • Other trains have fixed pricing. The number of tickets on sale is unlimited, and there is no seat reservation. In Benelux, InterCity and local trains of the same company have the same pricing structure (unlike Germany and France where InterCity and local trains are priced differently).
    • In the Netherlands and Belgium, you can use any train(s) (which do not require seat reservation) along the route indicated on the ticket, for a single journey or a return journey, throughout the day. The base fare of a return ticket is just two times the price of a single ticket. (There could be a separate ticket-issuing cost, which means that a return ticket can be marginally cheaper than two single tickets.)
    • In the Netherlands, there are some trajectories where more than one company runs trains; if there is a price difference, which there usually is, when you buy a ticket the machine/website/app will show you the price difference, and ask you to specify which company's train(s) you will catch (there is also a third option which allows you to take either option).
    • In Luxembourg, there is a time-based fare structure covering all modes of public transport (train, bus, tram; including foreign trains) within the country. There is a 2-hour ticket, and a day ticket, which is valid until 04:00 the following day.
  • For local train and Intercity direct/InterCity train travels between the Netherlands and Belgium, NS (Dutch rail) and NMBS/SNCB (Belgian rail) offer a fixed-rate discount for tickets bought seven or more days in advance from their international website or app. The discount is larger for journeys on Monday to Thursday. There is no seat reservation, and you can use any train during the day along the designated route (crossing a specific border point). The number of tickets is limitless; the discounted price remain the same seven or more days in advance, and the normal full-flex price remain the same throughout. The discount tickets are less-flexible than the normal full-flex tickets, in terms of changes and cancellations. No discount is given by the ticket machines. (This type of cross-border discount is not applicable to journeys between just Roosendaal and Belgium, or between just Maastricht and Belgium, as these are operated solely by NMBS/SNCB. The old day-return and weekend-return tickets between the Netherlands and Belgium were scrapped on 9th April 2018.)
  • Surcharges (no seat reservation is possible/needed for these):
    • For the Amsterdam–Berlin InterCity service, domestic travel within the Netherlands on this service is just like any other domestic InterCity services, and there is no surcharge.
    • For ICE, domestic travel within the Netherlands requires normal domestic ticketing plus an ICE surcharge of €2,40 (pre-purchased; in some cases this surcharge is waived). For domestic travel on the ICE within Belgium, the surcharge is €8 for second class, and €12 for first class (pay on board?).
    • For Intercity direct, there is a surcharge of €2,40 (pre-purchased; in some cases discounted) for the section between Schiphol Airport and Rotterdam Centraal in the Netherlands. This surcharge is not applicable to tickets including the Schiphol Airport–Rotterdam Centraal trajectory and to/from/through Belgium.
    • The station of Brussels Airport-Zaventem has a station access fee (the 'Diabolo fee') of €5,30, for each single access.
  • There are no longer regular long-distance night trains or autotrains in Benelux. The closest access points for international night trains are ÖBB (Austrian Rail) Nightjet from Germany (e.g. Hamburg, Hannover, Düsseldorf, Cologne, Frankfurt), and SNCF (French Rail) Intercités de Nuit from Paris-Austerlitz. Some ÖBB Nightjet trains can take cars/motorcycles. SNCF has a separate autotrain service from Paris-Bercy (no passengers). Not included in the maps are seasonal charter trains to, e.g., ski destinations in the winter (some of which are night trains). (ÖBB is interested in running night trains to the Netherlands, and this service might start in 2020.)

Developments on train travels in Benelux since 10-12-2017:

  • 01-01-2018: NRW-Tarif is now valid on RE 18 Heerlen – Herzogenrath.
  • 14-01-2018: RB 61 starts operating between Hengelo and Bad Bentheim. (In reality, replacement buses run till Sunday 25-02-2018.)
  • 12-02-2018: The station of Waddinxveen Triangel opens, on the Gouda – Alphen aan den Rijn line.
  • 20-02-2018: Eurostar announces its London–Amsterdam schedule from 04-04-2018.
  • 23-02-2018: NS announces that the non-peak surcharge-free period for Intercity direct is extended till 08-04-2018. (During this period, there is a surcharge for Intercity direct between Schiphol Airport and Rotterdam Centraal only between 06:30–09:30 and 16:00–19:00 on weekdays.)
  • 28-03-2018: The station of Eemshaven opens; Eemshaven is now the northern-most train station in the Netherlands.
  • 04-04-2018: Eurostar begins its operation in the Netherlands. However, border control in the Netherlands cannot start until at least the end of 2019; when passengers travel towards London, passengers have to disembark and go through Schengen and UK border controls in Brussels. The train towards London stops at Brussels for 1 hour 18 minutes; Eurostar usually put passengers on Thalys trains to get to Brussels.
  • 09-04-2018: IC Amsterdam – Brussels rerouted to high-speed lines. 12 trains per day run through the Schiphol–Rotterdam high-speed line to/from Amsterdam (these are now classed as Intercity direct in the Netherlands), while 4 trains per day goes to/from Den Haag HS (these remain InterCity). Dordrecht and Roosendaal are now skipped by the IC, while Breda and Noorderkempen are added.
  • 09-04-2018: Replacement buses for P/RE trains no longer run between Gouvy and Troisvierges.
