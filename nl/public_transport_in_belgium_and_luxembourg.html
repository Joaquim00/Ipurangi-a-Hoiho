Public transport in Belgium and Luxembourg

The following is a brief summary of the public transport situation in Belgium and Luxembourg.

Belgium

Belgium is the first country in Continental Europe to develop rail transport. The network of passenger rail in Belgium is denser than the Netherlands, but the frequency of services is somewhat inferior to the Netherlands. Belgium has invested a lot more into high speed rail than the Netherlands; Brussels is also a much more important international railway hub than any cities in the Netherlands.

There is one passenger railway company: NMBS/SNCB (Nationale Maatschappij der Belgische Spoorwegen) / Société nationale des chemins de fer belges). (Or in German, NGBE (Nationale Gesellschaft der Belgischen Eisenbahnen).) Printed time tables in Dutch, in French. Map of the 'line numbers'. Another official map of the network (2014).

Arne Nys' unofficial map of the Belgian passenger railway network (2015).

As for other surface public transport, there are many sub-companies and franchisees, but from a user's point of the view, there are basically three public transport providers in Belgium besides the railway company:

  • Flanders: De Lijn. Maps for each province: Antwerpen, (Belgian) Limburg, Oost-Vlaanderen, Vlaams-Brabant, West-Vlaanderen.
  • Brussels: STIB-MIVB. Network map, map of metro/pre-metro and Belgian rail S-trains in Brussels, night network map.
  • Wallonia: TEC. Timetables, maps for each operating area: Brabant Wallon, Charleroi, (no maps available for Hainaut), Liège-Verviers, Namur-Luxembourg.

There are buses, metros (Brussels), pre-metros (Brussels, Antwerp), light rail (Charleroi), trams (Brussels, Antwerp, Gent, Coastal Tram). The Coastal Tram is the world's longest tram line, and run nearly the entire length of the Flemish coast, from Knokke near the Dutch border to Adinkerke near the French border.

All four public transport providers have their own fare structure (see NMBS/SNCB, De Lijn, STIB-MIVB, TEC). However, they are moving towards using the MOBIB card, which is Belgium's contactless transport card. All four already use the MOBIB card for at least some subscriptions, but so far only STIB-MIVB and TEC have anonymous MOBIB cards, and only STIB-MIVB uses the MOBIB card for normal single trips (you load single-trip tickets to the MOBIB card, or bundels of 5 singles, 10 singles, or other products). In the future, you should be able to make single journeys on the four companies using MOBIB card.

On Belgian trains, you can buy train tickets on the train, but there is a surcharge of €7, i.e., they strongly want you to have a ticket before boarding a train. See what they say on the various ways of getting a train ticket.

Luxembourg

The national rail operator is CFL (Société Nationale des Chemins de Fer Luxembourgeois). Electronic network map, timetables, printed timetables. It also operates some bus routes.

There are three local bus companies:

  • Luxembourg City: AVL (Autobus de la Ville de Luxembourg). Timetables, printed maps: network map, night network map. Free bus.
  • Canton d'Esch [S and SW of Luxembourg City; second largest city]: TICE (Syndicat des Tramways Intercommunaux du Canton d'Esch). printed timetables and electronic maps.
  • Rest of the country: RGTR (Régime Général des Transports Routiers).

Detailed information on all four public transport providers can be obtained from the Mobilitéit website. The entire country runs on the same fare structure. Kuerzzäitbilljee is a 2-hour ticket, and Dagesbilljee is a 1-day ticket (which costs just two times a 2-hour ticket). AVL says that single 2-hour/1-day tickets can be bought from the bus driver, while bundels (discounted sets of ten 2-hour tickets, or five 1-day ticekts) are sold elsewhere. CFL says that there are ticket-machines at the train stations, and tickets from the machines are validated immediately. Tickets bought inside a train cost an extra €1.

Kuerzstreckekaart is a monthly short distance ticket. See this map for what is considered 'short distance'.

mTicket is the smart phone ticket, and mKaart is the contactless smart card for the whole country. Various tickets and subscriptions can be loaded to them.

What I have talked about above is the situation with domestic tickets; on various webpages, it is said that the domestic tickets are not valid to/from the border. Mobilitéit's page on international tickets says that Luxembourgian domestic tickets do cover some localities in Germany, Belgium, and France immediately across the border. For international travel to further places on Luxembourgian carriers, there are zone 1 and zone 2 tickets (map); a 1-day international ticket costs slightly less than two 2-hour international tickets.
