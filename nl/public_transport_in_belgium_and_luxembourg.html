Public transport in Belgium and Luxembourg

The following is a brief summary of the public transport situation in Belgium and Luxembourg.

Belgium

The following are airports with scheduled passenger flights in Belgium. (Also shown are their relative location to Dutch border localities, and to each other.)

  • Antwerp Airport ANR ( English | Dutch ). South of the Dutch border at Roosendaal. A mix of leisure flights, buisness-oriented flights, and other regional flights. You can catch bus 51, 52, and 53 from the airport to Antwerpen-Berchem station. From there, to the Netherlands, you can catch the Intercity Brussels north to Roosendaal and beyond. Or change at Antwerpen-Centraal for Thalys for even-faster service.
  • Ostend–Bruges Airport OST ( English | Dutch | French ). Soutwest of the Southwestern coast of the Netherlands. Mostly leisure flights. It looks like that it is possible to walk to the Coast Tram stops Oostende Raversijde (to the northwest) or Oostende Ravelingen (to the northeast) in 1.5 km or less. Otherwise, there is bus 6 to Oostende station from the airport (there are more buses on the main road). (There are also shuttles to Brugge.) From Oostende to the Netherlands, you can, e.g.,: a)catch a train to Brugge, then bus 42 to Breskens in Zeelandic Flanders (the Netherlands), and then ferry to Vlissingen, and from there to other parts of Zeeland; or b)catch a train to Gent-Sint-Pieters, then bus 50 to Terneuzen in Zeelandic Flanders, then other buses to elsewhere in Zeeland; or c)catch a train to Gent-Sint-Pieters, then train to Antwerpen-Centraal, then Intercity Brussels or Thalys north to the Netherlands.
  • Brussels Airport BRU ( English | Dutch | French ). South of Antwerp. Major international airport, similar in passenger numbers as Düsseldorf Airport. It has its own underground railway station Brussels Airport-Zaventem. From there, to the Netherlands, you can catch the Intercity Brussels north to Roosendaal and beyond. Or change at Antwerpen-Centraal for Thalys for even-faster service.
  • Charleroi Airport CRL ( English | Dutch | French ). South of Brussels, often marketed as Brussels South Airport. Major hub for low-cost and leisure carriers. You can catch bus A from the Airport to Charleroi-Sud train station, and from there north to Brussels. There is a combined ticket for Charleroi bus A and trains to any Belgian rail destinations. For the Netherlands, at Brussel-Zuid/Bruxelles-Midi, you can catch the Intercity Brussels north to Roosendaal and beyond, or Thalys for even-faster service to the Netherlands.
  • Liège Airport LGG ( English | Dutch | French | German ). South of the Dutch border at Maastricht. Leisure carriers. (Liège Airport LGG is much more important as a cargo airport than a passenger airport.) There is bus 57 (weekdays only) or bus 53 to Liège-Palais, where you can catch Belgian Rail IC (line 40) directly to Maastricht, or with a change at Liège-Guillemins.

Belgium is the first country in Continental Europe to develop rail transport. The network of passenger rail in Belgium is denser than the Netherlands. Belgium has invested a lot more into high speed rail than the Netherlands; Belgium has high-speed rail running in four directions from Brussels, whereas the Netherlands only has high-speed lines going towards the south. (But then the Netherlands is also a lot flatter; many ordinary lines can achieve respectably high speed for non-high-speed rail.) Brussels is also a much more important international railway hub than any cities in the Netherlands.

(That said, frequency of services in Belgium is inferior to the Netherlands. In the Netherlands, other than to Utrecht Maliebaan, you can expect at least two trains per hour with NS. In Belgium, there are two-hourly services with NMBS. Also the last train on Sunday can depart rather early, even from provincial capitals, as I regretfully found out...)

There is one passenger railway company: NMBS/SNCB (Nationale Maatschappij der Belgische Spoorwegen / Société nationale des chemins de fer belges). (Or in German, NGBE Nationale Gesellschaft der Belgischen Eisenbahnen.) Printed time tables in Dutch, and in French. Map of the 'line numbers'. Another official map of the network (2014).

Also very useful is Arne Nys' unofficial map of the Belgian passenger railway network (2015).

In Belgium, IC is Intercity, L is Lokale trein / train Local (all stop), P is a Piekuurtrein / train d'heure de Pointe (peak hour train), S is Suburban Brussels.

Besides the railway company, (from a user's point of the view) there are three public transport providers in Belgium:

  • Flanders: De Lijn. Maps for each province: Antwerpen (Antwerp), (Belgian) Limburg, Oost-Vlaanderen (East Flanders), Vlaams-Brabant (Flemish Brabant), West-Vlaanderen (West Flanders).
  • Brussels: STIB-MIVB. Printed network map, map of metro/pre-metro plus S-trains in Brussels, night network map.
  • Wallonia: TEC. Timetables, maps for each operating area: Brabant Wallon (Walloon Brabant), Charleroi, (no maps available for Hainaut), Liège-Verviers, Namur-Luxembourg.

There are buses, metros (Brussels), pre-metros (Brussels, Antwerp), light rail (Charleroi), trams (Brussels, Antwerp, Gent, Coastal Tram). The Coastal Tram is the world's longest tram line, and run nearly the entire length of the Flemish coast, from Knokke near the Dutch border to Adinkerke near the French border.

All four public transport providers have their own fare structure (see ticket options with NMBS/SNCB, De Lijn, STIB-MIVB, TEC). However, they are moving towards using the MOBIB card, which is Belgium's contactless transport card. All four already use the MOBIB card for at least some subscriptions, but so far only STIB-MIVB and TEC have anonymous MOBIB cards, and only STIB-MIVB uses the MOBIB card for normal single trips (you can load single-trip tickets to a MOBIB card, or sets of 5 singles, sets of 10 singles, or other products). In the future, you should be able to make single journeys on all four companies using the MOBIB card.

On Belgian trains, there is a surcharge of €7 for buying train tickets on the train, if you are inside Belgium. If you fail to buy a train ticket, you will get a fine. See what they say about the various ways of getting a train ticket.

(This says that the surcharge does not apply if you board a Belgian train in neighbouring countries. The page specifically mentions all the stations that Belgian local trains serve in the Netherlands (Maastricht, Maastricht Randwyck, Eijsden, and Roosendaal) and Germany (Aachen Hbf). It also mentions 'all stations in Luxembourg'. For France, it only mentions 'Lille'. I suppose it covers all stations from Lille-Flandres to the Belgian border, but it is unclear. The stations in France are (towards the northeast from Lille:) Lille-Flandres – Croix Wasquehal – Croix l'Allumette – Roubaix – Tourcoing, and (towards the east from Lille:) Lille-Flandres – Lezennes – Hellemmes – Pont de Bois – Annappes – Ascq – Baisieux.)

See Vrienden van de Voetveren for a list of ferries in Belgium (and also the Netherlands/Luxembourg).

Luxembourg

Luxembourg Findel Airport LUX ( English | French | German ). As a passenger airport, it has many flights to destinations in Europe, Anatolia, and the Maghreb. As a cargo airport, it is amongst the busiest cargo airports in Europe, with many long haul cargo flights. As for public transport from the airport, there is bus 16 (Eurobus) and bus 29 to the Central train station. There is also bus 117 to Trier (Germany), and many other shuttle services. (For trains to the Netherlands, you can take the faster train northeast to Brussels, or the slower train north to Liège, and then connect from there. See the international rail page.)

The national rail operator is CFL (Société Nationale des Chemins de Fer Luxembourgeois). See their electronic network map, timetables, and printed timetables. It also operates some bus routes.

There are three local bus companies:

  • Luxembourg City: AVL (Autobus de la Ville de Luxembourg). Timetables, printed maps (network map, night network map). There is a free park&ride bus (parking costs on weekdays).
  • Canton d'Esch [south and southwest of Luxembourg City; Esch is the second largest city]: TICE (Syndicat des Tramways Intercommunaux du Canton d'Esch). Printed timetables and electronic maps.
  • Rest of the country: RGTR (Régime Général des Transports Routiers).

Detailed information on all four public transport providers can be obtained from the Mobilitéit website. The entire country runs on the same fare structure. Kuerzzäitbilljee is a 2-hour ticket, and Dagesbilljee is a 1-day ticket (which costs just two times a 2-hour ticket). AVL says that single 2-hour/1-day tickets can be bought from the bus driver, while bundles of ten 2-hour tickets, or five 1-day ticekts are sold elsewhere. CFL says that there are ticket-machines at the train stations, and tickets from the machines are validated immediately. Tickets bought inside a train cost an extra €1.

Kuerzstreckekaart is a monthly short distance ticket. See this map for what is considered 'short distance': 'short distance' is six or less hexagons away from your origin; a group of yellow hexagons is considered one hexagon if it is your origin or your destination.

mTicket is the smart phone ticket, and mKaart is the contactless smart card for the whole country. Various tickets and subscriptions can be loaded to them.

What I have talked about above is the situation with domestic tickets; on various webpages, it is said that the domestic tickets are not valid to/from the border. On the other hand, Mobilitéit's page on international tickets says that Luxembourgian domestic tickets do cover some localities in Germany, Belgium, and France immediately across the border. For international travel to further places on Luxembourgian carriers, there are zone 1 and zone 2 tickets (map); a 1-day international ticket costs slightly less than two 2-hour international tickets.

Luxembourg is building a tram system, the Luxtram. CFL is building a funicular. There is a foot passenger/car ferry crossing the Mosel between Wasserbillig (Luxembourg) and Oberbillig (Germany) (it has its own fare structure).
