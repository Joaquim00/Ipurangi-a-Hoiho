Public transport in Belgium, Luxembourg, and nearby Germany

The following is a brief summary of the public transport situation in Belgium, Luxembourg, and parts of Germany closest to the Netherlands. (Plus a very little bit of France.) See also my Benelux train service network maps for graphical illustrations of some of the journey options raised below.

Belgium

The following are airports with scheduled passenger flights in Belgium. Also shown are their relative location to the Dutch border, and to each other.

  • Antwerp Airport ANR. South of the Dutch border at Roosendaal. A mix of leisure flights, buisness-oriented flights, and other regional flights. You can catch bus 51, 52, and 53 from the airport to Antwerpen-Berchem station. From there, to the Netherlands, you can catch the Intercity Brussels north to the Netherlands. Or change at Antwerpen-Centraal for Thalys for an even-faster service. There are also coach services from Antwerpen-Centraal to Eindhoven.
  • Ostend–Bruges Airport OST. On Flemish coast, soutwest of the southwestern coast of the Netherlands. Mostly leisure flights. There is bus 6 to Oostende station from the airport. Otherwise, there are more buses on the main road, and the Coastal Tram stops Oostende Raversijde and Oostende Ravelingen are within 1.5 km. There are also shuttles to Brugge. From Oostende or Brugge to the Netherlands, you can, e.g.: a) catch a direct train to Antwerpen Centraal, or to Gent- Sint-Pieters and then change to a train to Antwerpen Centraal, and then trains north to the Netherlands; b) from Brugge catch bus 42 north to Breskens in Zeelandic Flanders (in the Netherlands), ferry across to Vlissingen, and catch a Dutch train to somewhere else; c) at Gent-Sint-Pieters can bus 50 north to Terneuzen in Zeelandic Flanders (in the Netherlands), then catch some other Dutch bus elsewhere.    
  • Brussels Zaventem Airport BRU ( English | Dutch | French ). South of Antwerp. The primary airport of Belgium, similar in passenger numbers to Düsseldorf Airport. It has its own railway station Brussels Airport-Zaventem. From there you can catch the Intercity Brussels north to the Netherlands. Or change at Antwerpen-Centraal for Thalys for an even-faster service. Alternatively, catch a train to Leuven or Brussel-Noord/Bruxelles-Nord, then a train east to Liège-Guillemins, and then a train north to Maastricht.
    •    
  • Charleroi Airport CRL ( English | Dutch | French ). South of Brussels, often marketed as Brussels South Airport. Major hub for low-cost and leisure carriers. You can catch bus A from the Airport to Charleroi-Sud train station, and from there a train north to Brussels. There is a combined ticket for Charleroi bus A and trains to any Belgian rail destinations. You can get this ticket at any Belgian train stations. For the Netherlands, at Brussel-Zuid/Bruxelles-Midi, you can catch the Intercity Brussels north to the Netherlands, or Thalys for even-faster service. Alternatively, catch a train east to Liège-Guillemins, and then a train north to Maastricht.
  • Liège Airport LGG. South of the Dutch border at Maastricht. Leisure carriers. (Liège Airport LGG is much more important as a cargo airport than a passenger airport.) There is bus 57 (weekdays only) to Liège-Guillemins, or bus 53 to Liège-Palais, where you can catch Belgian Rail IC (line 40) directly to Maastricht on weekdays, or with a change at Liège-Guillemins during weekends.

Belgium is the first country in Continental Europe to develop a railway network. The railway network in Belgium is much denser than the Netherlands. Belgium has invested a lot more into high speed rail than the Netherlands; Belgium has high-speed rail running in four directions from Brussels, whereas the Netherlands only has high-speed lines going towards the south. (But then the Netherlands is also a lot flatter; many ordinary lines can already achieve relatively high speeds.) Brussels is also a much more important international railway hub than any cities in the Netherlands. (That said, the frequency of train services, and timetabling in general, is much better in the Netherlands than in Belgium.)

There is one passenger railway company: NMBS/SNCB (Nationale Maatschappij der Belgische Spoorwegen / Société nationale des chemins de fer belges). (Or in German, NGBE Nationale Gesellschaft der Belgischen Eisenbahnen.) Printed time tables in Dutch, and in French. Network map. My railway network maps of Benelux.

In Belgium, IC is Intercity, L is Lokale trein / train Local (all stop), P is a Piekuurtrein / train d'heure de Pointe (peak hour train), S is Suburban Brussels.

Besides the railway company, (from a user's point of the view) there are three public transport providers in Belgium:

  • Flanders: De Lijn. Maps for each province: Antwerpen (Antwerp), Belgian Limburg, Oost-Vlaanderen (East Flanders), Vlaams-Brabant (Flemish Brabant), and West-Vlaanderen (West Flanders). Antwerp Airport ANR is in Antwerpen, Ostend–Bruges Airport OST is in West-Vlaanderen, and Brussels Zaventem Airport BRU is in Vlaams-Bramant.
  • Brussels: STIB-MIVB. Network maps: Printed network map, map of metro/pre-metro plus S-trains in Brussels, and night network map.
  • Wallonia: TEC. Timetables, and maps for each operating area: Brabant Wallon (Walloon Brabant), Charleroi, Liège-Verviers, and Namur-Luxembourg (no maps available for Hainaut). Charleroi Airport CRL is in Charleroi, and Liège Airport LGG is in Liège.

There are buses, metros (Brussels), pre-metros (Brussels, Antwerp), light rail (Charleroi), and trams (Brussels, Antwerp, Gent, Coastal Tram) in Belgium. The Coastal Tram is the world's longest tram line, and run nearly the entire length of the Flemish coast, from Knokke near the Dutch border to Adinkerke near the French border.

All four public transport providers have their own fare structure (see ticket options at NMBS/SNCB, De Lijn, STIB-MIVB, TEC). However, they are moving towards using the MOBIB card, which is Belgium's contactless transport card. All four already use the MOBIB card for at least some subscriptions, but so far only STIB-MIVB and TEC have anonymous MOBIB cards, and only STIB-MIVB uses the MOBIB card for normal single trips (you can pre-load single-trip tickets to a MOBIB card, or sets of 5 singles, sets of 10 singles, or other products). In the future, you should be able to make single journeys on all four companies using the MOBIB card.

On Belgian trains, there is a surcharge of €7 for buying train tickets on the train, if you are inside Belgium. If you fail to buy a train ticket, you will get a fine. See what they say about the various ways of getting a train ticket.

(This says that the surcharge does not apply if you board a Belgian train in neighbouring countries. The page specifically mentions all the stations that Belgian local trains serve in the Netherlands (Maastricht, Maastricht Randwyck, Eijsden, and Roosendaal) and Germany (Aachen Hbf). It also mentions 'all stations in Luxembourg'. For France, it only mentions 'Lille'. I suppose it covers all stations from Lille-Flandres to the Belgian border, but it is unclear.)

Bicycle carriage on Belgian trains: folded folding bike can be taken for free, otherwise €5 one-way, €8 for the entire day in the entire Belgian network.

De Lijn talks about a bicycle ticket for the coastal tram. On STIB-MIVB, see what they say about bicycle carriage (basically, folding bicycles allowed; normal bicycles allowed, with restrictions, on low-floor metro and low-floor trains). TEC says that only folding bicycles are allowed on their vehicles.

There is the Euregio-ticket, which is a day ticket for up to two adults and three children, valid on weekends and holidays, covering all regular buses and many local trains in Liège Province, Belgian Limburg, southern Dutch Limburg, and the Aachen area in Germany.

See Vrienden van de Voetveren for a list of ferries in Belgium (and also in the Netherlands/Luxembourg).

Luxembourg

Luxembourg Findel Airport LUX ( English | French | German ). As a passenger airport, it has many flights to destinations in Europe, Anatolia, and the Maghreb. As a cargo airport, it is amongst the busiest cargo airports in Europe, with many long haul cargo flights. From the airport, there are bus 16 (EUROBUS) and bus 29 to the Central train station. There is also bus 117 to Trier (Germany), and many other shuttle services. (For trains to the Netherlands, you can take the faster train northeast to Brussels, or the slower train north to Liège, and then connect from there. See the international rail page.)

The national rail operator is CFL (Société Nationale des Chemins de Fer Luxembourgeois). See their timetables, printed timetables (domestic and regional, long-distance international), and electronic network map. It also operates buses to Lorraine TGV and Saarbrücken Hbf.

There are three local bus companies:

  • Luxembourg City: AVL (Autobus de la Ville de Luxembourg). Timetables, and printed maps (network map, night network map). There is a free park&ride bus (parking costs on weekdays).
  • Canton d'Esch [south and southwest of Luxembourg City; Esch is the second largest city]: TICE (Syndicat des Tramways Intercommunaux du Canton d'Esch). Printed timetables and electronic maps.
  • Rest of the country: RGTR (Régime Général des Transports Routiers).

Detailed information on all four public transport providers can be obtained from the Mobilitéit website. The entire country runs on the same fare structure. Kuerzzäitbilljee is a 2-hour ticket, and Dagesbilljee is a 1-day ticket (which costs just two times a 2-hour ticket). AVL says that single 2-hour/1-day tickets can be bought from the bus driver, while bundles of ten 2-hour tickets, or five 1-day ticekts are sold elsewhere. CFL says that there are ticket-machines at the train stations, and tickets from the machines are validated immediately. Tickets bought inside a train cost an extra €1.

Kuerzstreckekaart is a monthly short distance ticket. See this map for what is considered 'short distance': 'short distance' is six or less hexagons away from your origin; a group of yellow hexagons is considered one hexagon if it is your origin or your destination.

mTicket is a smart phone ticket, and mKaart is the contactless smart card for the whole country. Various tickets and subscriptions can be loaded to them.

What I have talked about above is the situation with domestic tickets. On various webpages, it is said that the domestic tickets are not valid to/from the border. On the other hand, Mobilitéit's page on international tickets says that Luxembourgian domestic tickets do cover some localities in Germany, Belgium, and France immediately across the border. For international travel to further places with Luxembourgian carriers, there are zone 1 and zone 2 tickets (map); a 1-day international ticket costs slightly less than two 2-hour international tickets.

On CFL trains and buses, bicycles can be taken for free.

Luxembourg is building a tram system, the Luxtram. CFL is building a funicular. There is a foot passenger/car ferry crossing the Mosel between Wasserbillig (Luxembourg) and Oberbillig (Germany) (it has its own fare structure).

Nearby Germany

The following are airports in Germany which have webpages in Dutch; having webpages in Dutch shows that they get a significant number of customers from the Netherlands, or Flanders.

  • Weeze Niederrhein Airport NRN ( English | Dutch | German ). On the Dutch border, approximately equidistant from Nijmegen and Venlo. Often marketed as Düsseldorf Weeze Airport. Low-cost and leisure carriers. For the Netherlands, there is Airport-Weeze-Shuttle (reservation required), which runs between Nijmegen station and Weeze Airport. There are also time-tabled services to Venlo station, and charter service to Arnhem Gelredome, of which the price per person decreases as the number of booked passengers increases. There is also AirExpressBus (reservation required) that goes Weeze Airport – Eindhoven Airport – Den Bosch Pettelaarpark – Utrecht P+R Westraven – Amsterdam Centraal. Even cheaper (check timetable): bus SW1 from Weeze Airport northeast to Weeze station, train RE10 from Weeze station north to Kleve station, and then bus SB58 from Kleve station west to Nijmegen station. You can buy a ticket for the whole way (price level C) from the German bus driver, in either direction (Nijmegen is Nimwegen in German.).
  • Düsseldorf Airport DUS ( English | Dutch | German ). West of Venlo and Roermond. Third busiest airport in Germany in terms of passenger numbers. There are two different train stations for this airport: Düsseldorf Flughafen Terminal (at the terminals; served by only S11), and Düsseldorf Flughafen (2.5km away; served by all other trains). Check very carefully which station you are meant to board your train at! Skytrain runs between Düsseldorf Flughafen Terminal and Düsseldorf Flughafen (check carefully whether your air/train ticket covers the Skytrain). For the Netherlands, from either train stations: a) make your way to Düsseldorf Hbf or Duisburg Hbf for ICE north to Arnhem Centraal – Utrecht Centraal – Amsterdam Centraal; or b) At Düsseldorf Flughafen or Düsseldorf Hbf catch RE19 north to Arnhem Centraal; or c) from Düsseldorf Hbf, RE13 west to Venlo; or d) from Düsseldorf Hbf, RE4 west to Herzogenrath and then RE18 west to Heerlen.
  • Cologne Bonn Airport CGN ( English | Dutch | German ). West of Maastricht/Heerlen. Another big airport: it handles less passengers than Düsseldorf, but more cargo than Düsseldorf. It has its own train station Köln/Bonn Flughafen. From there, you can: a) catch RE8 west to Mönchengladbach Hbf and then RE13 west to Venlo; b) make your way to Köln Hbf, then ICE/RE1/RE9 west to Aachen Hbf, then make your way north to Herzogenrath, and then RE18 west to Heerlen; or c) make your way to Köln Hbf, and then ICE north to Arnhem Centraal – Utrecht Centraal – Amsterdam Centraal; or d) catch RE6 and change at Düsseldorf Hbf or Düsseldorf Flughafen or Duisburg Hbf to RE19 north to Arnhem Centraal.
  • Dortmund Airport DTM ( English | Dutch | German | Polish ). Further west than Düsseldorf. Low-cost and leisure carriers. From the airport, you can catch the AirportExpress (bus) west to Dortmund Hbf, or can the AiportShuttle (bus) south to Holzwickede. For the Netherlands, from Dortmund Hbf you can: a) catch RB51 north to Enschede; or b) catch RE3 west to Oberhausen Hbf, or RE1, RE6, RE11 west to Duisburg Hbf, and then change to RE19 north to Arnhem Centraal; or c) catch RE4 to Herzogenrath, and then change to RE18 to Heerlen. From Holzwickede, you can catch train RE13 west to Düsseldorf Hbf and Venlo.
  • Münster Osnabrück Airport FMO ( English | Dutch | German ). West of Enschede. Low-cost and leisure carriers. You can catch, e.g.: a) bus S50 (fast), D50 or R51 south to Müster Hbf and catch RB64 from there northwest to Enschede; b) bus X150 northeast to Osnabrück and catch Intercity Berlin west to Hengelo and beyond; c) bus S50 north to Ibbenbüren, catch a train west to Rheine, and then Intercity Berlin west to Hengelo and beyond; or d) bus 252 southwest to Greven Radhaus, walk or bus west to Greven station, train northwest to Rheine, then Intercity Berlin west to Hengelo and beyond.

The dominant provider of passenger rail service in Germany is Deutsche Bahn 'German Rail'. There are also other smaller comapanies. The DB journey planner, www.bahn.com, is a very useful tool. Not only do they show journeys on DB trains, they also show journeys on trains of other companies, journeys on many other modes of public transport (including many ferries), and also connections to/from/within other European countries. (Their coverage of non-train journeys in foreign countries is not necessarily good, so do not rely on it totally for travel outside Germany.)

A distinction needs to be made between long-distance trains, and regional/short-distance trains. With DB, the fastest long-distance trains are called the ICE (Intercity-Express). The slower long-distance trains are called EC (EuroCity; international trains) or IC (Intercity; mostly domestic, some international trains).

For regional/short-distance trains, there are the faster IRE (Interregio-Express) and RE (Regional-Express). The slower regional/short-distance trains are called RB (Regionalbahn), which are usually all-stop trains. In larger urban areas, there is also the S-bahn, which is suburban rail service. (But some of these 'suburban' lines go very far.)

This long-distance versus regional/short-distance distinction is important; this is because there are many tickets which give unlimited usage of the regional/short-distance trains, plus all bus/trams/U-bahn (underground trains) etc. Tickets issued by local public transport authorities are typically also valid on the regional/short-distance trains.

Other than buying normal train tickets from DB, there are also many 'offers'. Amongst the many 'offers' are the 'regional offers'. There are the nationwide day-tickets for regional/short-distance trains: the Quer-duchs-Land-Ticket (weekday), and the Schönes-Wochenende-Ticket (weekend). Even better-value are the state-wide day tickets, the Länder-Tickets. Price of a one-person ticket is perhaps not a good deal, but it becomes a really good deal when you are a group of five adults travelling on the same nationwide/state-wide ticket. In addition, people can take their own young children/grand-children along for free with these tickets. Please read the conditions of the tickets very carefully before you purchase and use them. (I am not responsible if you misread the ticket conditions.)

In the links above, you might have read the term 'transport association'. A Verkehrsverbund '(public) transport association' is a geographically based association of all the public transport providers in an area. (Providers of bus-, tram-, U-bahn, S-bahn services and the like.) The aims are, amongst other things, to have a unified fare/ticket system, to coordinate their services, and to give unified information to the public.

Most transport associations have a unified fare structure. On top of this, there are some umbrella-fare-structures that are valid in several transport associations. For instance, in North Rhine-Westphalia (NRW), there are many tickets that are valid throughout the entire NRW state, including the services that they run in neighbouring areas, e.g. the German buses in Venlo and Nijmegen. The bicycle day-ticket in NRW is also a NRW-wide ticket. The public transport operators in most districts in Germany fall into a transport association. I will call the operating area of a transport association a 'public transport region'. The following is a map of the public transport regions in Germany from German Wikipedia. (The shades show various types of transport associations; areas without a transport association is in light grey.)

Bicycle carriage rules differ greatly with different public transport providers; in general, the word to search for in websites is Fahrradmitnahme (or just Fahrrad 'bicycle'). (NRW has unified rules; see below). With DB, this is their page on Bahn und Fahrrad 'Train and bicycle'. With long-distance trains, bicycle carriage is 'generally not possible' on ICE (but see what Dutch Rail says on the carriage of bicycles on the ICE to/from the Netherlands), but possible on EC and IC (with a bicycle ticket, on domestic trains). For regional/short-distance trains (IRE, RE, RB, S-bahn), it is different in different states.

From this point onwards, I will only talk about the public transport regions in western Germany closest to Benelux. I will discuss them from north to south, and from west to east.

Niedersachen 'Lower Saxony'

There is the Niedersachsen-Ticket 'Lower Saxony Ticket' (DB website in English, in German, information from its own website in German). (Read the clauses very carefully!) There is also a Niedersachsen-Ticket-plus-Groningen which is very good value (page in German, Dutch). Here are other good deals in Lower Saxony from the DB page in German (see the navigation bar on the left). An interactive map of the train lines in Lower Saxony can be found here.

In Lower Saxony, the following public transport regions are closest to the Netherlands. (I think none of these transport associations have tickets that include regional/short-distance trains):

  • VEJ – Verkehrsverbund Ems-Jade. Borders Groningen Province of the Netherlands. Ostfriesland 'East Frisia' plus the Jade Bay area to the east. Includes all the German Wadden/East Frisian Islands. Printed bus timetables and maps. Links to the ferry services to the German Frisian islands.
  • bvE – Busverkehr Emsland-Mitte/Nord. Borders Groningen and Drenthe Provinces of the Netherlands. Printed timetables.
  • VGC – Verkehrsgemeinschaft Landkreis Cloppenburg. To the east of the aforementioned bvE region. Map.
  • VGB – Verkehrsgemeinschaft Grafschaft Bentheim. Borders Drenthe and Overijssel Provinces of the Netherlands. Printed timetables and maps.
  • VGE – Verkehrsgemeinschaft Emsland-Süd. To the east of the aforementioned VGB region. Printed timetables and map (click on the map to get printed timetables).
  • VOS – Verkehrsgemeinschaft Osnabrück. To the east of the aforementioned VGE region. A large part of the Osnabrück region juts into North Rhein-Westphalia (NRW), and there are many NRW tickets that are valid in some parts of the Osnabrück region. Electronic timetables, and electronic network map.
  • VGV – Verkehrsgemeinschaft Landkreis Vechta. Northeast of the VOS region, and southeast of the VGC region. Printed timetables (you might have to scroll back up a little bit to see the full range).

Nordrhein-Westfalen 'North Rhein-Westphalia' (NRW)

There is the SchönerTagTicket NRW 'Beautiful Day Ticket North Rhine-Westphalia'. (In English, in German; Read the ticket clauses very carefully!) Here are other good deals in NRW from the DB page in German (see the navigation bar on the left). Here is a website for buses and trains in NRW: busse-und-bahnen.nrw.de. Information on NRW-tickets, a printed network map, and an electornic map of regional/short-distance train network in NRW. (In the printed network map: NRW-issued day/seasonal tickets are valid on the dotted lines, but not valid on the black lines. ALL lines to/from the Netherlands are dotted lines, except the line between Heerlen and Herzogenrath, which is black. The line between Aachen and Belgium is also black.)

Information on taking a bicycle on trains and buses in the North Rhine-Westphalia. (It is generally allowed in trains and buses, with some restrictions. Folded-up folding bicycles are considered luggage.)

In North Rhein-Westphalia, the following public transport regions are closest to the Netherlands and Belgium. (The tickets of these transport associations include the usage of regional/short-distance trains):

  • VGM – Verkehrsgemeinschaft Münsterland. Borders Overijssel and Gelderland Provinces of the Netherlands. Timetables, map of regional/short-distance rail network, and maps of each Stadt and Kreis. Münster Osnabrück Airport FMO is in Kreis Steinfurt.
  • VRR – Verkehrsverbund Rhein-Ruhr (English). Borders Gelderland and Limburg Provinces of the Netherlands. Printed timetables of RE/RB/S-bahn, printed system map of RE/RB/S-bahn, and printed maps of each city/district. Düsseldorf Airport DUS is in Düsseldorf, Weeze Niederrhein Airport NRN is in Kleve District, and Dortmund Airport DTM is in Dortmund.
  • AVV – Aachener Verkehrsverbund. Borders Limburg Province of the Netherlands, and Liège Province of Belgium (mostly German-speaking parts of Liège Province). Printed timetables, and printed maps: rail network, rail network of Euregio Maas-Rhein (AVV area, southern Dutch Limburg, Belgian Limburg, Liège Province), Aachen region, Düren region, Heinsberg region, and many others. There is the Euregioticket, which is a weekend/holiday day ticket maximally two adults and three children, covering all regular buses, and many local trains in the tri-national region (see the brochure).
  • VRS – Verkehrsverbund Rhein-Sieg (English). Borders Liège Province of Belgium (German-speaking parts of Liège Province). East and south of the AVV area. Printed timetables, printed rail network maps, and printed bus network maps. Cologne Bonn Airport CGN is in Köln 'Cologne'.

Rheinland-Pfalz und Saarland 'Rhineland-Palatinate and Saarland'

The Rhineland-Pflaz-Ticket 'Rheinland-Palatinate-Ticket' and the Saarland-Ticket are the same thing; they are both valid in both Rheinland-Palatinate and Saarland. Saarland has a number of special cross-border tickets, including the Saar-Elsass-Ticket to Alsace (the weekend 5-person day-ticket is good deal), and the Saar-Lor-Lux-Ticket, a weekend day-ticket that covers Saarland (Germany), Lorraine (France), and the Grand Duchy of Luxembourg.

A network map of the rail network can be found here (or here, and then press Liniennetzkarte).

In Rhineland-Palatinate, there is one transport region that borders Belgium and Luxembourg. (Their tickets include the usage of regional/short-distance trains):

  • VRT – Verkehrsverbund Region Trier (English). Borders Liège Province of Belgium (German-speaking parts of Liège Province), and Luxembourg. Printed timetables, map of the whole region, and printed network map of Trier.

There is one transport region in Saarland. (Their tickets include the usage of regional/short-distance trains):

  • saarVV – Saarländischer Verkehrsverbund (Français). Borders Luxembourg, and Département Moselle of France. Journey Planner.
