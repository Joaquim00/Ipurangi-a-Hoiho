International passenger rail services

The Netherlands has some international rail links. Railway is important to the Netherlands, and the Netherlands has a good and functional rail network. Nonetheless, given that the Netherlands shares land border with only two countries, and that the North Sea sea–rail connection is much less popular now than it was in the steam era, the Netherlands' role as an international rail hub is not as prominent as it was in the past. (The sea–rail connection between England and Germany via the Netherlands can still be done, although you have to change trains at least twice in the Netherlands.) Brussels and German cities like Düsseldorf and Cologne are more important international rail-hubs than the cities in the Netherlands. The following lists direct international passenger rail services to/from the Netherlands.

Long distance international rail services

See timetables from NS International, or printed time tables from NS.

To Germany and beyond:

  • Intercity Berlin: Amsterdam Centraal – Hilversum – Amersfoort – Apeldoorn – Deventer – Almelo – Hengelo –[border]– Bad Bentheim – Rheine – Osnabrück Hbf – [Bad Oeynhausen or Bünde (Westfalen)] – Minden (Westfalen) – Hannover Hbf – Wolfsburg Hbf – Stendal – Berlin-Spandau – Berlin Hbf – Berlin Ostbahnhof. Locomotive and crew change at Bad Bentheim. (Hbf: Hauptbahnhof 'main station'.)
  • ICE International, high-speed: Amsterdam Centraal – Utrecht Centraal – Arnhem Centraal –[border]– Oberhausen Hbf – Duisburg Hbf – Düsseldorf Hbf – Köln Hbf – Frankfurt (Main) Flughafen Fernbahnhof ✈ – Frankfurt (Main) Hbf, and once per day further to Basel SBB (Switzerland). (Köln is Cologne).
  • Night trains: current direct destinations are Munich (Germany) and Zürich (Switzerland). It is unclear how much longer there will be night trains to/from the Netherlands, as Deutsche Bahn (German Rail) will no longer operate night trains from the end of 2016. In any case, there will be night trains operated by, e.g., ÖBB (Austrian Rail), to/from nearby places like Cologne or Düsseldorf, where you can easily connect to/from the Netherlands.

To Belgium and beyond:

  • Intercity Brussels: Amsterdam Centraal – Schiphol Airport ✈ – Den Haag Hollands Spoor – Rotterdam Centraal – Dordrecht – Roosendaal –[border]– Antwerpen-Centraal – Antwerpen-Berchem – Mechelen – Brussels Airport-Zaventem ✈ – Brussel-Noord/Bruxelles-Nord – Brussel-Centraal/Bruxelles-Central – Brussel-Zuid/Bruxelles-Midi. At Brussel-Zuid/Bruxelles-Midi, other than changing trains to other Schengen Area destinations, you can also go through immigration and customs and catch the Eurostar to SE England. For Luxembourg, changing at Brussel-Noord/Bruxelles-Nord is quicker than the other Brussels stations. (Antwerpen is Antwerp; Brussel in Dutch and Bruxelles in French is Brussels.)
  • Thalys, high-speed: Amsterdam Centraal – Schiphol Airport ✈ – Rotterdam Centraal –[border]– Antwerpen Centraal – Brussel Zuid/Bruxelles-Midi –[border]– [Paris Nord or Lille Europe]. During summer, there are weekly direct trains to Marseille-Saint-Charles on the southern coast of France. During winter, there are weekly direct trains to Bourg-Saint-Maurice in the French Alps.

There are no longer car-trains to/from the Netherlands. The closest places you can get car-trains are probably Düsseldorf, Hamburg, and Paris. There are also the Eurotunnel Le Shuttle car-trains across the English Channel.

Across the Dutch-Belgian border (from east to west):

  • NMBS/SNCB Line 40: Maastricht – Maastricht Randwyck – Eijsden –[border]– Visé – Bressoux – Liège-Guillemins, and on weekdays further to Hasselt. Printed timetables: Line 40. Printed timetables: Line 12.

If you travel internationally frequently by ordinary train, and if you have a subscription with NS (or similar subscriptions with railway companies in many other European countries), then getting RailPlus (press the 'RailPlus' tab in that page) could be advantageous. (But then, if you are travelling from the Netherlands to Belgium, the Super Day Return and the Weekend Return are even cheaper. Press the 'Prices' tab in this.)

It is best to get e-tickets online through the websites of the national railway companies. In some countries they have dedicated websites for international train travels e.g. NS International. A great number of train ticket machines in the Netherlands can issue tickets to Belgium (including Lille in France), Luxembourg, and many train stations in Germany (mostly the ones closer to the Dutch border, and certain bigger ones further away, perhaps Hamburg, Hannover, Frankfurt, and Berlin). Ticket counters at larger train stations can probably deal with train tickets to Belgium and Luxembourg, and perhaps Germany (i.e. basically the same destinations as the ones you can buy at a machine). Dedicated international ticket counters can handle the full range of international train tickets, but they are found in only seven strategic train stations in the Netherlands. In addition, getting tickets through ticket counters incur an extra service charge, and that can be hefty.

There are other railways from the Netherlands to Germany or Belgium, but they are only used by freight trains, or in limited cases, tourist trains.

See also Public transport in Belgium, Luxembourg, and nearby Germany, especially for Belgian and German local trains departing from the Netherlands.

International bicycle carriage rules

  • Bicycle carriage rules on international trains to/from the Netherlands.
  • Belgian trains: folded folding bike can be taken for free, otherwise €5 one-way, €8 for the entire day in the entire Belgian network.
  • Germany: you have to check with individual public transport providers... in general the word to search for in their websites is Fahrradmitnahme. With DB, this is their page on Bahn und Fahrrad 'Train and bicycle'. With long-distance trains, it is 'generally not possible' on ICE, and possible on IC and EC trains (with a bicycle ticket, on domestic trains). For regional/short-distance trains (IRE, RE, RB, S-bahn), it is different in different states...
