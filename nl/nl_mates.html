The Netherlands and its neighbours.

A little lesson on the Netherlands and its neighbours, with many links from Wikipedia. (Information in Wikipedia is not necessarily accurate!)

The Netherlands, Holland, etc.

The Netherlands is a country. The adjectival form, and its people and language, are called Dutch in English. The Netherlands consists of the 12 provinces in European Netherlands and the 3 special municipalities in Caribbean Netherlands. The 12 provinces in European Netherlands are:

  • Groningen
  • Drenthe
  • Fryslân (Friesland in Dutch)
  • Overijssel
  • Flevoland
  • Gelderland
  • Utrecht
  • Noord-Holland
  • Zuid-Holland
  • Zeeland
  • Noord-Brabant
  • Limburg

and the 3 special municipalities in Caribbean Netherlands are:

  • Bonaire
  • Sint Eustatius
  • Saba

(The special municipalities have the functions of municipalities, and some functions of provinces.) The pages here in How to NL are mainly about European Netherlands, while Caribbean Netherlands will also be touched upon. (I will try to learn more about the Caribbean Netherlands in the near future.)

The governing body above the Netherlands is The Kingdom of the Netherlands, which is a sovereign state. The Kingdom of the Netherlands consists of 4 constituent countries: The Netherlands, Aruba, Curaçao, and Sint Maarten. The latter three are entirely in the Caribbean. While Caribbean Netherlands and European Netherlands have basically the same laws, Aruba, Curaçao, and Sint Maarten have their own legal systems. The only matters shared amongst the four consituent countries are things like nationality laws (i.e. they have the same Dutch nationality), foreign affairs, and defence. Aruba, Curaçao, and Sint Maarten are basically not discussed here in How to NL, as they are not The Netherlands.

(Another term you might encounter is Dutch Caribbean, which includes Caribbean Netherlands, Aruba, Curaçao, and Sint Maarten.)

The Netherlands had a huge colonial empire. In the 20th century, the ex-colonies became Indonesia (SE Asia), and Suriname (S America). Many Dutch people are originally from, or have ancestors from, these places. Dutch is still spoken by most people in Suriname, and many Dutch words have entered the Indonesian language and other languages spoken in Indonesia.

In the 19th century, Belgium gained independence from the Netherlands, and Luxembourg was in a personal union with the Netherlands (the king of the Netherlands was also the grand duke of Luxembourg). These three soverign states have been – since 1940s – part of the Benelux Union, which share many policies.

(Also in the 19th century, British Gold Coast absorbed Dutch Gold Coast and Danish Gold Coast; in the 1950s, British Gold Coast, together with British Togoland, gained independence as Ghana. See here for a history of the Dutch empire.)

The Netherlands is often informally referred to as Holland; Holland is in fact a region of the Netherlands (now the provinces of Noord-Holland and Zuid-Holland), albeit the most prosperous one in the country. The using of Holland to refer to the entire Netherlands is somewhat similar to how England is informally used to refer to the entire United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland. Nonetheless, Dutch people are often not offended when their country is referred to as Holland. They sometimes do that themselves, especially in international sports context. The official tourism website of The Netherlands is called Holland.com.

The capital of the Netherlands is Amsterdam. Important royal functions, e.g., coronation, are hold in Amsterdam. The Hague ('s-Gravenhage or Den Haag in Dutch) is not (called) the capital; it is the seat of government, and embassies of foreign countries are also in The Hague.

The Randstad is one of the largest Megalopolis in Europe, both in terms of population and economic importance. It consists of Amsterdam, The Hauge, Rotterdam, Utrecht, and smaller cities in between. It contains the port of Rotterdam, the largest seaport in Europe, and Amsterdam Schiphol airport, one of the largest airports in Europe.

The official language of the Netherlands is Dutch. Other than the Kingdom of the Netherlands, Dutch is also an official language in Belgium and Suriname. The Afrikaans language in South Africa and Namibia is largely mutually intelligible with Dutch. The majority of Dutch people speak very good English, and are usually willing to speak English to foreigners. (Be courteous; don't just assume that everyone has to speak English with you.) Many also speak German and/or French.

Regional languages with official status are (West) Frisian in Fryslân, Papiamentu in Bonaire, and English in Sint Eustatius and Saba. Other than Frisian, the European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages also recognises the following regional languages in the Netherlands: Dutch Low Saxon, Limgurgish, Romani, and Yiddish. (See also: Dutch dialects.)

Traditionally, the dominant religion in European Netherlands is Calvanistic Protestantism, while Roman Catholicism also has very strong following. The Netherlands was roughly divided into a Protestant north, and Catholic south. These days, most people are not very involved in religion, except in the (Protestant) Bible Belt, which run roughly from the southwest to the northeast.

The Netherlands is known for its liberalism (or perhaps, pragmatism) in many of its policies. For example, the Netherlands is the first country in the world to legalise same-gender marriage. (Same-gender marriage is also available in Caribbean Netherlands. Same-gender marriage is not performed in Aruba, Curaçao, and Sint-Maarten, but marriage performed in the Netherlands is recognised by them.) Drugs are illegal, but there is an official policy of non-enforcement for private usage of drugs that cause relatively low harm. (See, e.g., Drug policy of the Netherlands.) The Dutch government is more concerned about your health, than policing the usage of 'soft' drugs.

Note that in the Netherlands, a coffeeshop, spelt in English, is a place where you can smoke cannibis/marijuana. A place where you can drink coffee is a café. Although coffeeshops are tolerated by the national government, local governments can place extra restrictions on them. For instance, many municipalities, especially the ones near the German or Belgian border, do not allow coffeeshops to serve people not residing in the Netherlands.

Abortion and prostitution are legal in the Netherlands. With prostitution, again, local councils can place restrictions on prostitution venues. 'Window' prostitution, which the Netherlands is famous for, can be found only in about 10 cities. (See, e.g., Prostitution in the Netherlands.)

Nearby countries

European Netherlands borders Germany to the east, and Belgium to the south.

The Federal Republic of Germany consists of 16 constituent states. The two states that border Netherlands are Niedersachsen (Lower Saxony) in the north, and Nordrhein-Westfalen (North Rhine-Westphalia) to the south. Nordrhein-Westfalen is the most populous state of Germany, and the Rhine-Ruhr area, close to the Netherlands, is another large and important Megalopolis in Europe.

The German empire ended in 1918, when it became a federal republic; at the end of World War I in 1918, Germany lost all its colonies. The German language is a West Germanic language like Dutch. German has official status at national level in six countries: Germany, Austria, Liechtenstein, Switzerland, Luxembourg, and Belgium. Outside of Europe, the German language is most visible in Namibia, one of Germany's ex-colonies. See also German dialects.

The Kingdom of Belgium is a federal constitutional monarchy. There is a national government, but much of the power is devloved to the various sub-national governments. Most importantly, there is the governments of the three regions: Flanders, Wallonia, and the Brussels-Captial Region. Flanders is mostly Dutch-speaking. Wallonia is mostly French-speaking, with a German-speaking region in the extreme east. Brussels is officially bilingual in French and Dutch, but in reality mostly French speaking. Other than these three sub-national governments, there is also the Government of the French Community, and the Government of the German-speaking Community. (The government of Flanders is also the government of the the Dutch-speaking Community.) Belgium did not suffer badly during its 589 days without an elected federal government in 2010–2011, because so much power is devolved to the various sub-national government.

Language is a delicate political issue in Belgium. Historically, the minority French language had much more political power than the majority Dutch language. These days, the Flemish government and Wallonian government make little concessions for each other's language. For example, on road signs in Flanders, names of places in Wallonia (and France) often only appear in their Dutch version, and similarly in Wallonia, names of places in Flanders often only appear in their French version. Sometimes the Dutch place name and the French place name of the same place can look very different. For example, the Wallonian city of Mons is known as Bergen in Dutch. The Flemish city of Antwerp is known as Antwerpen in Dutch, but Anvers in French. For foreigners travelling in Belgium, it is advisable to be versed in the placenames in Belgium, and also nearby regions, in both Dutch and French, and perhaps also German. For example, the French city of Lille (which was originally Flemish) is known as Rijsel in Dutch. The German city of Aachen is known as Aken in Dutch, but Aix-la-Chapelle in French. The Wallonian city of Liège is known as Luik in Dutch, and Lüttich in German.

In the 1960s, the ex-Belgian colonies gained independence as (what are now known as) Democratic Republic of the Congo, Rwanda, and Burundi. They all have French as their official language, or one of their official languages. (Dutch did not have prestige in Belgium at that time.)

The official languages of Belgium are Dutch, French, and German. Belgian Standard Dutch is phonetically noticeably different from Netherlands Standard Dutch, and there are some lexical differences from Netherlands Standard Dutch, but grammatically they are both based on the urban dialects of Holland. 'Flemish' can refer to either Belgian Standard Dutch, or the local Flemish dialects of Dutch. The same can be said of the relationship between Belgian Standard French and French Standard French, and 'Wallonian'.

Belgium is traditionally Roman Catholic. However, these days few people are actively involved in religion. Flanders are somewhat more religious than Brussels and Wallonia.

Belgium is also relatively socially progressive. Belgium was the second country in the world to legalised same-gender marriage. The preceding prime minister is openly gay (2016). The possession of a very small ammount of cannibis/marijuana is legal in Belgium, but it is not as openly sold in the Belgium as in the Netherlands.

The Grand Duchy of Luxembourg is sandwiched between Belgium, Germany, and France. After the napoleonic era, the kings of the Netherlands were also the grand dukes of Luxembourg. After King William III of the Netherlands died in 1890, due to differences in the succession laws of the Netherlands and Luxembourg, the throne in the Netherlands was passed to his daughter Queen Wilhelmina, but in Luxembourg to his [17th cousin once removed through male line, or wife's half-uncle] Grand Duke Adolphe. The royal families of Luxembourg, Belgium, and the Netherlands are very close to each other in multiple ways.

The official languages of Belgium are Luxembourgish, French, and German. Unlike Belgium, where language use is mostly geographically-based, in Luxembourg basically all citizens are multilingual in at least three languages; all three languages are introduced before secondary school. The law is in French.

Luxembourg is somewhat less socially-progressive than the Netherlands and Belgium. Drugs are illegal. Same-gender marriage was legalised much later (2015). Nonetheless, currently (2016; since December 2013), the prime minister and the deputy prime minister of Luxembourg, from two different political parties, are both openly gay; basically no one in Luxembourg cares about the sexual orientation of politicians.

To the south of Belgium and Luxembourg is the French Republic. France has 18 regions, with 13 of them in Europe (also called Metropolitan France), and 5 of them overseas, situated in the wider Caribbean region, or off the East African coast. France also has eight other overseas territories, which have different sets of laws than the 18 regions of France proper (the 18 regions are often referred as la France entière 'the whole of France' by the French government). The official language is French.

Close to France and Belgium, across the English Channel, is England of the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland. The de facto official language of UK is English, the language that you are reading in.

Visa policies, border checks, and other things

European Netherlands is within the Schengen Area. The Schengen Area also includes Belgium, Germany, Luxembourg, Metropolitan France, and other countries. Within the Schengen Area, there are no systematic border checks on the borders amongst the member states. However, you could still be stopped at or near the border and asked for ID by the police, just like how you can be asked anywhere else in the country. In the Netherlands, it is compulsory to show you ID in certain situations (see what the government says on this issue).

Dutch Caribbean, and Overseas France, are outside the Schengen Area. You migh want to have a look at these (these are unofficial sources): visa policy of the Schengen Area, visa policy of Dutch Caribbean, visa policy of Overseas France.

United Kingdom is outside the Schengen Area. In some cases, you go through British border checks in France/Belgium before you board your Britain-bound vehicle, and vice versa for French border checks in Britain (Juxtaposed controls; please check official sources elsewhere for most-up-to-date information):

  • Eurotunnel Le Shuttle, for both immigration and customs clearance: a) French exit check and British entry check in France; b) French entry check in Britain.
  • Eurostar, for immigration clearance only: a) Belgian/French exit check and British entry check at Brussel-Zuid/Bruxelles-Midi, Paris-Nord, Lille-Europe, or Calais-Fréthun; b) French entry check in Britain (France acts for Belgium). (There can be customs check again at arrival. Britain-bound Eurostar trains from other French stations go through British border checks in Britain.)
  • Ferries to/from Dover (P&O Ferries, DFDS Seaways), for immigration clearance only: a) French exit check and British entry check at Calais or Dunkerque (Dunkirk); b) French entry check at Dover. (There can be customs check again at arrival.)

UK exit checks are not performed like entry checks; when leaving UK, staff of the train/ferry company checks details of your ID against information you provided them when you book your ticket.

United Kingdom is part of the Common Travel Area together with Republic of Ireland, Isle of Man, Jersey, and Guernsey. You migh want to have a look at these (these are unofficial sources): visa policy of the United Kingdom, Isle of Man, Jersey, and Guernsey, and visa policy of the Republic of Ireland. British territories overseas do not belong to the Common Travel Area; you might want to see (unofficial): visa policy of the British Overseas Territories.

European Netherlands uses the Euro € (together with, e.g., Belgium, Luxembourg, Germany, France proper, Republic of Ireland), whereas Caribbean Netherlands uses the US dollar $. European Netherlands uses the Central European Time (UTC+1) in winter, and Central European Summer Time (UTC+2) in summer (time in Europe). Caribbean Netherlands uses the Eastern Caribbean Time (UTC–4) year-round.
