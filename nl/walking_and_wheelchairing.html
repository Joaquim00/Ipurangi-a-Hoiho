Walking and wheelchairing

Walking is often a pleasant way to explore the Netherlands. Wandelen is recreational walking. Many places have signposted walks that you can follow. (Signage is often – but not necessarily – good. Bring your own GPS, especially for walks outside urban areas.)

Wandelnet.nl has various information on the wandelpaden in the Netherlands, including the national LAW's (Lange-Afstand-Wandelpaden 'long distance hiking paths') which cover the length of the entire country. An electronic map by them, but for only some parts of the country. Wandel.nl is also another good website on wandelen in the Netherlands. Wandelkalender.nl shows walking events. Amongst the 'hardcore' walking events, the Vierdaagse in Nijmegen is the most famous. It takes place in July each year, and it attracts a huge crowd of walkers, many from foreign countries. During four days, each day walkers walk on designated routes of 30/40/50/55 km (which categories you are allowed join depends on your age and gender), and there is also a military category. Sort out accomodation in Nijmegen (or surrounding areas) well in advance. During that week, there is extra public transport to/from/within the entire Arnhem-Nijmegen area, both for the walkers commuting from elsewhere, and for partygoers to/from Nijmegen at night.

Another popular activity is wadlopen 'mudflat hiking'. It is possible to walk across to some of the Wadden Sea islands (some of the Frisian islands) during low tide. Of cause there is more risk waddling in the mud than walking on dry land, so only go with an experienced guide. A list of companies offering such tours can be found here. The Waddenwandelen.nl website itself offers information on walks on the Northern mainland coast, on the Wadden Sea Islands, including Borkum (German).

Wheelchair accessibility seems to be not too bad in the Netherlands, in comparison with many other countries. (Of cause there is more room for improvement.) This is what Holland.com and Angloinfo.com say about accessible travel in the Netherlands. Accessibletravelnl.com is a company specialising in accessible travel, and, e.g., see their page of rental of bicycle-type vehicles that are suitable for people with special accessibility needs. (Handbikes are not uncommonly seen in the Netherlands.)

For both walkers and wheelchairers, the Netherlands and the rest of Continental Europe have right-hand traffic. Remember to look left first, and then look right, before crossing the road, if it is clear on both sides. Strips of road surface with red bricks, or painted in red-brick colour, are often bicycle paths; bicycles can run pass in high speeds. Cyclists can come in from both directions. Look out carefully for tram tracks. Take care at train level crossings.

The emergency number in the Netherlands is 112. (Methods for deaf/less-hearing people; read this well now before you need it.) 112 is also valid in other countries of the European Union. (GSM phones from other countries might also recognise the common emergency telephone numbers in other countries, and then redirect that to 112 if you are in European countries. But do not rely on that.)
