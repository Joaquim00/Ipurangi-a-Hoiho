How to go to/from/within The Netherlands

This page was originally intended to show only network maps on the internet. It then started to get a life of its own.

This page is about some ways of going to/from/within The Netherlands. If you just want to know how to get from A to B within the Netherlands by public transport, 9292.nl (app: Google Play, Apple App Store, BlackBerry World.) The following are aimed at network maps, and other related travelling information.

I try to link to official sournces of information as much as possible. However, I cannot guarantee the accuracy of the information that I or they provide. Information can easily go out of date.

The Netherlands, Holland, etc.

The Netherlands is made up of the 12 provinces in European Netherlands and the 3 special municipalities in Caribbean Netherlands: Bonaire, Sint Eustatius, and Saba. This page is mainly about European Netherlands, while Caribbean Netherlands will be touched upon. Not discussed here are the three other constituent countires of the Kingdom of the Netherlands in the Dutch Caribbean: Aruba, Curaçao, and Sint Maarten, which have their own legal systems.

European Netherlands is within the Schengen Zone, so theoratically there are no border checks on the land borders with Germany and Belgium, or with flights from other places within the Schengen Zone. (But then occationally you can be stopped at or near the border and asked for ID by police, just like you can be asked anywhere else in the country.) Caribbean Netherlands is outside the Schengen Area, similar to French départements d’outre-mer. See also these unofficial sources: visa policy of the Schengen Area, visa policy of the Dutch Caribbean. European Netherlands uses the Euro €, whereas Caribbean Netherlands uses the US dollar $.

The Netherlands is often informally referred to as Holland; Holland is in fact a region of the Netherlands (now the provinces of Noord-Holland and Zuid-Holland), albeit the most prosperous one in the country. The using of Holland to refer to the entire Netherlands is somewhat similar to how England is often informally used to refer to the entire United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland. (But then Dutch people not from Holland are usually not offended when their country is referred to as Holland. They do that themselves, especially in international sports context.)

Passenger flight

The primary gateway of the Netherlands is Amsterdam Schiphol Airport AMS. In the last few years, it has been the fifth or sixth busiest airport in the world in terms of international passenger numbers. The passenger numbers through Schiphol dwarfs all the other airports in the Netherlands. It has its own underground train station, with direct international trains to Belgium and France, and to Germany via easy connection elsewhere on the Dutch railway network.

The second busiest airport is Eindhoven Airport EIN. It is a large base for low-cost and leisure carriers, offering many short haul flights to Europe, Anatolia, and Maghreb.

The third busiest airport is Rotterdam The Hauge Airport RTM. Other than budget and leisure carriers, the airport also attracts some traditional carriers, with some of the routes buisness-oriented.

Two smaller airports with regular passenger flights in European Netherlands are Groningen Airport GRQ in the northwest, and Maastricht Aachen Airport MST in the southwest.

Other options include the airports in nearby countries. Many are a quick drive from the Dutch border, and some have good public transport links to the Netherlands. The closest airports are the ones in the Rhine-Ruhr area and Belgium:

  • Weeze Airport NRN. On the Dutch border, approximately equidistant from Nijmegen and Venlo. Often marketed as Düsseldorf Weeze Airport. Low-cost and leisure carriers.
  • Düsseldorf Airport DUS. West of Venlo and Roermond. Third busiest airport in Germany in terms of passenger numbers. Be very careful that there are two different train stations for this airport: Düsseldorf Flughafen Terminal, and Düsseldorf Flughafen. Check very carefully which one you are meant to board your intended train at.
  • Cologne Bonn Airport CGN. West of Maastricht/Heerlen. Another big airport, with its own train station (Köln/Bonn Flughafen).
  • Dortmund Airport DTM. Further west than Düsseldorf. Low-cost and leisure carriers.
  • Münster Osnabrück Airport FMO. West of Enschede. Low-cost and leisure carriers.

  • Antwerp Airport ANR. South of Roosendaal. A mix of leisure flights, buisness-oriented flights, other regional flights.
  • Ostend–Bruges Airport OST. Soutwest of the Southwestern coast of the Netherlands. Mostly leisure flights.
  • Brussels Airport BRU. South of Antwerp. Major international airport, similar in passenger numbers as Düsseldorf Airport. It has its own underground railway station.
  • Charleroi Airport CRL. South of Brussels, often marked as Brussels South Airport. Major hub for low-cost and leisure carriers.
  • Liège Airport LGG. South of Maastricht. Leisure carriers. (It is a much more important as a cargo airport than a passenger airport.)

The 'threat' from the Rhine-Rhur airports are strong enough that KLM provides two free buses pre day to/from Schiphol for customers in Arnhem/Nijmegen and Eindhoven/Maastricht.

In Caribbean Netherlands, each of the three islands has its own airport:

  • Bonaire Airport BON. Direct flights from Amsterdam and some places in USA. Otherwise flights are mostly to neighbouring Curaçao CUR, and sometimes Aruba AUA, which are much larger airports.
  • Sint Eustatius Airport EUX is small. You can get there from Sint Maarten SXM.
  • Saba Airport SAB is very small. You can get there from Sint Maarten SXM.

    Using Intercity Direct and international trains domestically

    • Intercity Direct (Amsterdam Centraal – Schiphol Airport – Rotterdam Centraal – Breda Centraal): Surcharge of €2.40 for everyone travelling between Schiphol Airport and Rotterdam Centraal.

    • Intercity Berlin: Domestic travel on this train in the Netherlands (between Amsterdam Centraal and Hengelo) is just like other domestic journeys.
    • Intercity Brussels. Domestic travel on this train in the Netherlands (between Amsterdam Centraal and Roosendaal) is just like other domestic journeys.
    • ICE International: For domestic travel in the Netherlands (Amsterdam Centraal – Utrecht Centraal – Arnhem Centraal), there is a surcharge of €2.40, unless you already have a subscription with NS, in which case there is no surcharge.
    • Thalys: totatally different fare structure of its own. No Dutch domestic tickets works with Thalys.

    Domestic bicycle carriage rules

    Within the Netherlands, folded-up folding bicycles are treated as normal luggage, and can be taken for free in all hours in domestic trains. For normal bicycles, all smaller operators seem to have the same rules as Nederlands Spoorwegen (including Breng), except for most lines on Arriva. NS bicycle day-tickets are valid in all domestic services listed below.

    • Nederlands Spoorwegen: Bicycles allowed outside rush hour (06.30–09.00, 16.00–18.30), whole day during weekends and holidays, and the entirety of July and August, with a bicycle day-ticket. Allowed on ICE travelling domestically (Amsterdam Centraal – Utrecht Centraal – Arnhem Centraal). Not allowed on Intercity Direct (highspeed Amsterdam Centraal – Schiphol Airport – Rotterdam Centraal – Breda Centraal).
    • Breng: Same as NS.
    • Syntus: Same as NS.
    • Valleilijn: Seems to be the same as NS.
    • Veolia: Same as NS.
    • Arriva:
      • Groningen and Friesland (train lines to/from Groningen or Leeuwarden): Other than the normal NS bicycle day-ticket, there is a cheaper Arriva-only bicycle day-ticket, valid only for Arriva trains in this region. The time of validity is also different: the Arriva bicycle day-ticket is valid from 09:00.
      • Vechtdallijnen (Zwolle – Emmen, Almelo – Mariënberg): Same as NS.
      • Gelderland (train lines to/from Zutphen or Arnhem): Same as NS, except that bicycles can be taken for free during the weekend.
      • MerwedeLingelijn (Geldermalsen – Dordrecht): free/gratis! Train can take maximally 12 bicycles.

    Cycling

    No country in the world has better cycling infrastructure than the Netherlands and Denmark. In the Netherlands, motorists are themselves cyclists; the Dutch motorist association, ANWB, started off as a cyclist club, and is still majorly involved in cycling issues. Looking out for cyclists is a prominent part of driver training in the Netherlands.

    One thing that is absent(?) in the Netherlands is road-side bicycle hires. But then, that is not really needed in the Netherlands; people already have multiple bicycles parked at various train stations, or they ride all the way to everywhere, or they have a folding bicycle that they take on trains, or they have a subscription with OV-fiets, or something like that. There are many shops that tourists and other people can hire a bicycle from.

    Bicycle paths are ubiquitous. Amongst the bicycle paths is the bicycle junction network fietsknooppuntennetwerk, or fietsroutenetwerk. Important bicycle path junctions are given numbers, and the numbers are signposted along the routes of the network (see examples in the Dutch Wikipedia article). The network was first built in northern Belgian Limburg, the network now covers the entirety of Flanders and Netherlands. The network extends into some bordering parts of Germany, and parts of Wallonia have joined. (Most prominently, Western part of Hainaut Province, the East Cantons (Eupen-Malmédy) of Liège Province (i.e. German-speaking Belgium plus some neighbours). Parts of the Namur and Luxembourg Provinces of Belgium have little networks that are not yet joined to the main network. See Dutch Wikipedia article.) (Prominently absent from all this is the Brussels-Capital Region.) Many guidebooks and digitial guides give recommended scenic routes in strings of bicycle junction numbers. There are numerous websites and apps that show the network, with some also allowing you to draw your own map by clicking/pressing the numbers in the order that you want. One Dutch example website, another Dutch website, a Belgian website.

    Bicycle helmets are largely worn only by children, professionals and other 'fast' cyclists, and foreign tourists. Netherlands has the second best bicycle safety record in Europe after Denmark (in terms of deaths per cycled distance). Some reading on cycling in the Netherlands.

    Fietsenstalling is a bicycle parking garage/facility. There are many public ones all around the country, most of them free. A bewaakte fietsenstalling is one that is watched by an attendent. Typically, when you enter with a bicycle, a ticket is attached to the bicycle, and a corresponding ticket is given to you. You then park your bicycle inside (you have to use your own bicycle lock). When you leave, you have to show the ticket, which matches with the ticket attached to the bicycle. The attendent will then take your ticket, and remove the ticket attached to the bicycle, and then you are free to go. Fietsenstallingen typically do not allow overnight parking. Do not leave valuables with the bicycle, and invest in a good bicycle lock: bicycle theft is a common occurence in the Netherlands.

    See above for the carrying of bicycles on international trains, and above on domestic trains. Carrying of bicycles on buses is generally not allowed in the Netherlands, unless it is a (small) folded-up folding bicycle. Trying searching for fiets meenemen (bicycle along-take) on the websites of the individual public transport providers.

    Car-pooling

    Car pooling is popular in the Netherlands, for both domestic and international trips. (Sorry I don't have a lot of experience on this; you'll have to do your own search online for carpooling companies/platforms.)

    Car-sharing

    Autodelen. This is an option if you stay in the Netherlands long enough, if you don't own a car. There are two types.

    With the first type, you become a member of a company, you go to their website/app, and book a vehicle for a period of time. Vehicles are placed at street locations. There is some special method to open the car at the beginning and lock the car at the end. You are charged by the amount of time and distance.

    • Greenwheels has the largest fleet in the Netherlands. Many are placed near train stations. You have to return the vehicle back to its dedicated parking spot at the end of the ride.
    • ConnectCar is similar to Greenwheels. Cars available in bigger cities in the west of the country.
