Benelux passenger rail network maps

The following are network maps of regular passenger train services in Belgium, the Netherlands, and Luxembourg in 2018. Except for the first general overview map, different types of train services are presented on separate maps. Having them on separate maps makes them less messy and much easier to follow than the existing everything-in-one maps. However, the disadvantage is that... they are on separate maps; you might have to switch between maps to get a better idea on the interconnectivity between stations. There are separate maps for weekday vs. weekend schedules; the difference is more noticeable with the Belgian network.

There are no longer regular international night trains or autotrains in Benelux. Not included in the maps are seasonal charter trains to, e.g., ski destinations in the winter (some of which are night trains).

Unlike Germany, tickets for InterCity train services in Benelux cost the same as local train services per distance. The same tickets are used for both InterCity and local train services; over the same route, if there is a choice between InterCity and local train services of the same company, you can use either service. Ticket machines should tell you if there is a price difference between different routes and/or trains of different companies to the same destination, in which case you are restricted to a particular route/company, unless you choose the option which allows you to take either train options. Breaking of journey is allowed within the same day (usually till 04:00 the following day, according to the scheduled departure time).

On 9 April 2018, Dutch Railways scrapped its weekend-return and day-return ticket deals to Belgium (for non-highspeed rail services). Ticket machines sell standard-price tickets. The website and app of NS International give an unlimitied-available and fixed-rate early-booking discount for the ICd service (through the Breda–Noorderkempen border) if booked at least 7 days ahead of the travelling date. (No discount for the local train services through the Roosendaal–Essen border or the Eijsden–Visé border.)

High speed rail services have different pricing structures, and are much more expensive than other rail services. There are some exceptions. In the Netherlands, a) ICE between Amsterdam Centraal and Arnhem Centraal, and b) Intercity direct between Schiphol Airport and Rotterdam Centraal, cost just the normal domestic price plus a prepaid surcharge of less than 3 euro (and the surcharge is waived in some cases). (The other sections of Intercity direct has no surcharge.) Between Brussels and Paris, IZY is a low-cost alternative offered by Thalys, and it runs parts of its distance on high-speed rail, and parts of its distance on conventional rail. Except for Intercity direct in the Netherlands and ICE used domestically within the Netherlands, tickets for high-speed rail services are usually restricted to particular services, i.e. you cannot join a train service earlier or later than the one specified on the ticket. With some, seating reservation is obligatory.

Developments since 10-12-2017:

  • 01-01-2018: NRW-Tarif is now valid on RE 18 Heerlen – Herzogenrath.
  • 14-01-2018: RB 61 starts operating between Hengelo and Bad Bentheim. (In reality, replacement buses run till Sunday 25-02-2018.)
  • 12-02-2018: The station of Waddinxveen Triangel opens, on the Gouda – Alphen aan den Rijn line.
  • 20-02-2018: Eurostar announces its London–Amsterdam schedule from 04-04-2018.
  • 23-02-2018: NS announces that the non-peak surcharge-free period for Intercity direct is extended till 08-04-2018. (During this period, there is a surcharge for Intercity direct between Schiphol Airport and Rotterdam Centraal only between 06:30–09:30 and 16:00–19:00 on weekdays.)
  • 28-03-2018: The station of Eemshaven opens; Eemshaven is now the northern-most train station in the Netherlands.
  • 04-04-2018: Eurostar begins its operation in the Netherlands. However, border control in the Netherlands cannot start until the end of 2019, so when passengers travel towards London, passengers have to disembark and go through Schengen and UK border control in Brussels. The train towards London stops at Brussels for 1 hour 18 minutes; Eurostar usually put passengers on earlier Thalys trains to get to Brussels first.
  • 09-04-2018: IC Amsterdam – Brussels rerouted to high-speed rail. 12 trains per day terminates at Amsterdam C, and 4 trains per day terminates at Den Haag HS. Dordrecht and Roosendaal are now skipped by this IC.
  • 09-04-2018: Replacement buses for P/RE trains no longer run between Gouvy and Troisvierges.
